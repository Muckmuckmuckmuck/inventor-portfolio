Overview

HelioRelay is a constellation of small, low cost satellites in low Earth orbit. Each one carries a large thin film mirror that catches sunlight and steers it down to a chosen spot on the ground, so a farm or a solar site can keep receiving light after the sun has dropped below its local horizon.

The satellites fly in a dawn-dusk orbit that keeps them lit by the sun while the ground below sits in twilight. As a satellite passes over a subscribed site, it angles its mirror to throw a pool of reflected sunlight onto that site for a short window. A fleet of these satellites hands the job from one to the next, so the light at a given farm can be extended through the most useful part of dawn and dusk.

What it is, stated plainly

This is not a second sun and it does not try to be. The honest physics, covered in the feasibility section, caps how bright and how sharp the spot can be. HelioRelay is built to do one thing cheaply and well: extend usable light at the edges of the day, for crops that respond to day length and for solar farms that lose money the moment the sun sets.

The problem being solved

Light on the ground runs out at fixed times, and a lot of value is lost in the gap.

  • Crops are limited by day length. Many plants grow and flower on cues from how long the light lasts. In high latitudes and in winter, short days slow growth and shrink the season.
  • Solar farms go dark first and last. Output collapses at dusk and is slow to start at dawn, exactly when grid demand often peaks. The panels sit idle while the need is high.
  • Greenhouses burn money on grow lights. Artificial lighting to extend the day is expensive and runs on grid power, which is often fossil fueled.
  • Frost lands in the pre-dawn dip. The coldest, most damaging moment for many crops is the hour before sunrise, when no sunlight is available to soften it.

HelioRelay attacks the timing problem. The sun is always shining somewhere above the horizon line of space. A mirror in orbit can borrow a little of that light and place it where the ground has run out.

Detailed description

Each satellite is deliberately simple, because cost per unit is the whole game when you need a fleet. The design is five subsystems wrapped around one big mirror.

1. Reflector membrane

A large, very thin film coated with a mirror finish, held flat by light booms under tension. The film is the kind of aluminized polymer already flown on solar sails. It is cheap by the square meter, folds into a small volume for launch, and weighs very little, which keeps the whole satellite light and cheap to loft.

2. Spacecraft bus

A small standardized satellite body in the CubeSat class. It holds the avionics, power, and pointing hardware. Standard buses are mass produced, so this is the part of the design that benefits most from buying off the shelf rather than inventing.

3. Pointing and attitude control

The hard part of this invention is aiming. The satellite has to rotate so the mirror sends its reflected beam at a specific moving spot on the ground while the satellite itself races overhead. This is done with reaction wheels for fine rotation, a star tracker and GNSS receiver to know exactly where it is and how it is oriented, and small thrusters to hold and adjust the orbit.

4. Onboard targeting computer

A small flight computer carries the list of subscribed ground sites and their coordinates. As the satellite approaches a site, it computes the mirror angle that lands the beam on target, accounting for the sun position, the satellite position, and the curve of the Earth, then drives the wheels to hold that aim through the pass.

5. Constellation and ground control

No single satellite can light a site for long, so HelioRelay is a fleet. A ground control system tracks every satellite, takes light requests from sites, and schedules which satellite serves which site and when, handing the job along as satellites move in and out of range.

How a single pass works

  1. A farm requests a light window, for example the forty minutes before local sunrise during the growing season.
  2. The scheduler finds which satellites will fly through a usable geometry over that site in that window, and reserves them.
  3. Each chosen satellite receives the site coordinates and the exact times it is responsible for.
  4. As a satellite comes into range, its computer works out the mirror angle that lands the beam on the field, and the reaction wheels slew to hold that aim while the satellite moves overhead.
  5. The spot sweeps across or sits near the field for a minute or two, then the satellite hands off to the next one rising behind it.
  6. Telemetry reports back what was delivered, which feeds billing and the next schedule.
Honest limit on the window

A satellite at this altitude is only over a given site for a few minutes per pass. Sustained light needs many satellites, so coverage and cost scale together. This is the central trade of the whole system, and it is why the design fights so hard to make each satellite cheap.

Potential applications

  • Photoperiod extension for crops. Adding light at the start or end of the day to nudge growth and flowering in plants that respond to day length.
  • Dawn and dusk boost for solar farms. Stretching the generating window at the exact hours the grid is most stressed.
  • Greenhouse supplement. Cutting the hours that electric grow lights have to run, which lowers cost and emissions.
  • Frost edge mitigation. A modest pre-dawn light and warmth boost over orchards on marginal nights.
  • Polar and high latitude sites. Places where winter days are short stand to gain the most from borrowed light.
  • Disaster and off-grid lighting. Temporary light over an area that has lost power, scheduled on demand.

Technical feasibility analysis

The pieces have flown before. The Soviet Znamya experiments reflected a moving spot of sunlight onto the night side of Earth in the 1990s, and thin film sails like IKAROS and LightSail proved that large mirror membranes deploy and survive in orbit. So the building blocks are real. What is hard is delivering useful intensity, pointing precisely, and doing both at a price that works.

The cost-effective version

The cheap, buildable path is not one giant mirror. It is many small, mass produced satellites, each with a modest membrane and an off the shelf bus, launched as rideshare into a dawn-dusk orbit. Value comes from selling short, scheduled light windows to sites that gain a lot from a little light at the right minute, like solar operators at dusk and high latitude growers in spring. Start with one demonstrator, prove the pointing, then scale the fleet only as paying demand appears.

Improvements and variations

  • Bigger membranes. Move from a 10 metre to a 30 metre film to raise delivered light by an order of magnitude per satellite, once deployment is trusted.
  • Spectral tuning. Coat the film to favour the red and blue bands that drive photosynthesis, so more of the delivered light is useful to plants.
  • Shared buses. Carry the same reflector payload on a standard commercial bus to cut development cost further.
  • Energy first model. Sell to solar operators before agriculture, since a solar farm turns even faint extra light directly into measurable revenue.
  • Dimmable by design. Slightly defocus on command to spread a softer, larger spot for crops, or tighten for a brighter, smaller one for energy.
  • Self disposal. Size the membrane so atmospheric drag pulls a failed satellite down quickly, leaving no long lived debris.

Prototyping roadmap

PHASE 0 · MODEL

Prove the numbers

Build the full optical and orbital model, confirm spot size, intensity, and pass windows, and find the sites where a little light is worth the most.

PHASE 1 · GROUND

Deployment and pointing rig

Deploy a film and boom assembly on the ground, and test the aiming software against a moving target on a gimbal. No launch yet.

PHASE 2 · DEMO SAT

One satellite in orbit

Fly a single demonstrator on rideshare. Measure the real spot on the ground with light sensors, and validate the pointing budget.

PHASE 3 · PILOT

Small cluster, paying site

A handful of satellites serving one solar farm or research plot, with real delivery data and a real customer.

PHASE 4 · SCALE

Fleet to match demand

Add satellites only as subscriptions grow, keeping the constellation sized to paying coverage.

Safety, ecology, and law

Putting light where nature removed it is not a neutral act, and a reflector fleet sits in a sky that many people share. These concerns are part of the design, not an afterthought.

  • Astronomy comes first. Bright reflective satellites can ruin observations. The system must publish its schedule, avoid pointing near observatories, and keep mirrors edge on when not working so they stay dim.
  • Night ecology is fragile. Artificial light at night disrupts insects, birds, and nocturnal animals. Light windows should be short, targeted, and studied for local effect before they become routine.
  • Eye safety holds by physics. Because the spot can never exceed natural daylight intensity, it cannot be focused into a harmful beam. That is a built in safety property, not a setting.
  • No new debris. Satellites must deorbit cleanly at end of life. Low mass film helps, since drag pulls it down and it burns up rather than lingering.
  • Shared sky, shared rules. Who is allowed to light whose ground, and across which borders, needs clear governance. This is as much a policy problem as an engineering one.

Risks and open questions

Is the light worth it?

The honest open question. The delivered intensity is low, so the whole idea depends on finding uses where a small, well timed boost still pays. That has to be proven with real data, not assumed.

Pointing under real conditions

Holding the beam on a field while moving at orbital speed, through every disturbance, is the toughest engineering risk.

Public acceptance of a brighter sky

Astronomers and dark sky advocates have real and reasonable objections. The project has to engage them early or it will be fought.

Unit economics at scale

Cheap per satellite still adds up across a fleet. The business only works if short light windows are valuable enough to many sites.
