Overview

VisionGrip is an advanced prosthetic arm for above-elbow and below-elbow amputees. Instead of asking the user to drive every small movement through muscle signals, the arm works as a semi-autonomous partner.

It combines neural intent sensors at the residual limb with a dense array of micro cameras. The cameras read the immediate environment while the intent sensors read what the user wants to do. When a user reaches for a basketball, the cameras track the ball's size, distance, and speed, and the hand moves into the correct catching posture and grip pressure before contact is made. The user supplies the goal. The arm works out the precise mechanics to reach it.

The core idea

Most prosthetic control fails because it forces a person to consciously manage dozens of small joint movements. VisionGrip moves that burden to the machine. The person aims and commits. The hand handles the geometry.

The problem being solved

Today's myoelectric prosthetics read muscle signals from the residual limb and map them to a small set of grips. That approach has real limits, and many users abandon their devices because of them.

  • High mental load. The user has to consciously trigger and hold each movement through muscle contractions. Simple tasks take constant attention.
  • Noisy, coarse signals. Surface muscle readings drift with sweat, fatigue, and electrode shift. Fine control is hard to sustain.
  • Slow grip selection. Switching between grip types often means cycling through a menu of patterns, which is too slow for anything fast or unexpected.
  • No awareness of the object. The hand does not know if it is about to hold an egg or a hammer. The user has to supply all of that judgment manually.
  • Abandonment. Studies of upper-limb prosthetics report that a meaningful share of users stop wearing their devices, often citing effort and limited function.

VisionGrip targets the root cause. If the hand can see the object and infer intent, it can take over the fine motor planning that currently exhausts the user.

Detailed description

The system is organized into five subsystems that pass data around a single closed loop. Each is described below, and the schematics in the next section show how they physically sit on the arm.

1. Visual sensory array

High framerate micro cameras are embedded in the fingertips, the palm, the back of the hand, and the wrist. The fingertip and palm cameras give close range detail for the final grip. The wrist camera gives a wider view for reaching and for tracking fast moving targets. Together they map the immediate space, estimate an object's size and likely fragility, and follow moving objects.

2. Intent sensory array

A non-invasive electromyography band, or a neural interface band, sits at the attachment site. It does not try to decode every finger. It reads the user's base intention, such as reach, pull, open, or commit to a grasp. This coarse signal is reliable and fast, which is exactly what the planner needs.

3. Processing unit

An onboard edge AI chip sits in the forearm cavity. It fuses the visual data with the intent data and runs predictive models of the grasp. Doing this on the device, rather than in the cloud, keeps latency low and keeps camera images private to the limb.

4. Actuation system

High torque micro servos drive synthetic tendons that move each digit. Independent digits and a rotating wrist let the hand form many grip shapes and adjust grip pressure smoothly rather than in fixed steps.

5. Haptic feedback loop

Small vibration motors at the attachment site let the user feel when an object is held securely. This closes the loop back to the person, so the user keeps a sense of contact and confidence even though the machine is doing the fine work.

Control modes

Autonomy is adjustable. The user is never locked out. Three modes trade speed for control, and the user can move between them.

Manual

The hand responds only to direct intent signals. Useful for training, for unusual tasks, and for users who want full control.

Assisted

The default. The user aims and commits, and the hand shapes the grip and sets pressure. The user can override at any point.

Autonomous reach

For fast or repetitive tasks. Once the user commits to a target, the hand tracks it and completes the grasp on its own.

Control authority

A hard rule sits under all three modes. A sustained intent signal from the user always overrides the planner. The machine handles geometry, but the person keeps the final say.

Potential applications

  • Daily living. Cooking, dressing, and handling fragile or oddly shaped items with far less conscious effort.
  • Sport and recreation. Catching, throwing, and gripping equipment where timing is too fast for manual grip selection.
  • Skilled and occupational work. Tool handling and assembly tasks that need consistent, repeatable grips.
  • Pediatric use. A scaled version that lowers the learning curve for children, who often struggle with effortful myoelectric control.
  • Rehabilitation data. The sensor stream is a rich record of how a user moves, which can guide therapy.
  • Platform for other limbs. The same vision and fusion stack extends to prosthetic feet, exoskeletons, and assistive robots.

Technical feasibility analysis

The honest read is that the building blocks exist, but packing them into a hand sized, all day device is the real challenge. Camera guided prosthetic hands and shared control have both been shown in research settings, which means the concept is not speculative. The hard work is integration, power, and trust.

Latency budget

For the hand to be ready before contact, the loop from camera frame to finger motion has to stay short. A rough target: capture and pre-process under 20 ms, inference under 60 ms, grasp planning under 40 ms, and actuation start under 60 ms. That keeps the system well under a fifth of a second, which is faster than a typical reach.

Improvements and variations

  • Richer intent. Pair the surface band with implanted muscle sensors or targeted muscle reinnervation for finer, more natural intent signals.
  • Real sensory return. Replace simple vibration with targeted nerve stimulation, so the user feels texture and force closer to natural touch.
  • Bone anchored mounting. An osseointegrated mount improves comfort, load transfer, and signal stability over a socket.
  • Modular hands. Quick swap end effectors, including tool specific ends, on the same intelligent forearm.
  • Shared grasp library. Grips learned across users improve the library over time, with all camera data processed on device to protect privacy.
  • Gaze assist. Optional AR glasses let the system know where the user is looking, which sharpens target selection in cluttered scenes.

Prototyping roadmap

A staged path keeps each step testable. Early phases use off the shelf parts to prove the idea before committing to custom hardware.

PHASE 0 · BENCH

Prove grip selection

An off the shelf robotic hand, a webcam, and a single board AI computer. Goal: show the system picks the right grip from camera input on a set of common objects.

PHASE 1 · INTEGRATE

3D printed forearm

Bring cameras, compute, and actuation into one printed forearm with a single grip. Validate the latency budget end to end.

PHASE 2 · ARTICULATE

Full hand and fusion

Independent digits, EMG fusion, force sensing, and haptic feedback. Measure grip success on fragile and moving objects.

PHASE 3 · WEAR

User trials

Fit testing with amputee volunteers under ethics review. Focus on comfort, trust, durability, and daily living tasks.

PHASE 4 · HARDEN

Design for manufacture

Cost reduction, sealing, reliability, and the start of the regulatory pathway.

Safety and ethics

  • The user stays in command. Autonomy assists, it does not take over. A clear override is always present, and failure should default to a safe, open hand rather than a clenched one.
  • Always on cameras raise privacy questions. The arm carries cameras that face the world. All image processing happens on the device, images are not stored or sent off by default, and the user controls any data sharing.
  • Medical device duty of care. Anything that attaches to a person and moves under power needs rigorous safety testing, clear failure behavior, and clinical validation before real use.
  • Access and cost. A device that only the wealthy can afford does not solve the problem at scale. Cost has to be a design goal, not an afterthought.

Risks and open questions

Trust calibration

How much autonomy feels helpful rather than unsettling? The right balance likely differs per user and per task, so it has to be adjustable and learnable.

Intent and action mismatch

If the hand acts on a predicted grasp and the user changes their mind mid reach, the system must cancel cleanly and fast.

Vision in the real world

Low light, glare, clutter, and occlusion all degrade camera input. The fallback to manual control has to be seamless.

Energy reality

Running cameras, an NPU, and motors all day is demanding. Battery life may force trade-offs in how often the system runs at full capability.
